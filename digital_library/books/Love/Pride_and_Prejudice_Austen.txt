PREFACE BY A LATE LITERARY GENTLEMAN.

The public are respectfully informed that the Author of these volumes
has only to request the civil attention of the candid and impartial
reader. She does not come before him, in the character of a compiler
of other men's observations, or a compiler of abstruse philosophy, but
as an original writer presenting observations on life and manners. The
work may be perused by those who wish to know more of the manner of
passing time in England some hundred and fifty years ago, and of the
customs of the female sex of a period when women were less educated,
less independent, and more constrained in their movements, than they
are in the present day.


A SLIGHT MEMOIR OF THE AUTHOR

Jane Austen was born at Steventon, in Hampshire, England, on December
16, 1775. She was the seventh child of the Rev. George Austen, and was
very much the pet of her parents and her six brothers and sister. She
was educated by her mother and also had the benefit of the instruction
of a clergyman, her uncle. From early girlhood she developed a taste
for writing, and her youthful compositions are characterized by that
combination of wit and satire which was to make her novels so popular.

Her father's death took place in 1805, and the family moved to Bath,
and afterward to Chawton in Hampshire. The latter place became the
scene of her literary activity, and it was here that she revised and
prepared for publication her novels, some of which had been written
years before.

"Pride and Prejudice" was first written under the title of "First
Impressions," and was offered by her father to a London publisher, but
was declined. This novel was the second of her works to be published,
and has always been the favorite with the reading public.

Jane Austen died on July 18, 1817, at Winchester, in Hampshire,
England, at the age of forty-one years and seven months. The death was
sudden and unexpected, and the cause is attributed by some to a severe
cold, and by others to Addison's disease. She was buried in Winchester
Cathedral.

Her novels are known for their accurate descriptions of the English
country life of her times, and for their witty and accurate sketches of
the characters which portray the society of her age. It has been said
that "Jane Austen was the greatest master of that branch of the novel
which depicts the social and domestic life of England; her works are to
the novelist what the works of Shakespeare are to the dramatist."
